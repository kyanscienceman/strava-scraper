To isolate the causal effect of wearing the Nike Vaporfly 4% on running performance is a difficult question. Many other factors that determine marathon finish time are correlated with the choice to buy Vaporflies. (For example: it is plausible that runners who choose to spend $250 on Vaporfly shoes are likely to be more commited to training for marathons.)

Controlling at the individual level does eliminate much of the variation, although we should still not expect an individual-level regression to be perfect by any means. With that in mind, we chose to estimate the equation:

\[\log(time)_{it} = \beta_0+\beta_1Vaporfly_{it}+A_i+U_{it}.\]

The subscript \(i\) varies across individuals, and the subscript \(t\) varies across the different years in which that individual ran a marathon. \(time_{it}\) represents marathon finish time, which we log transform since the rate of marathon time improvment is not linear. \(Vaporfly_{it}\) is an indicator variable for wearing Vaporfly shoes. \(A_i\) is a fixed effects parameter to control for any variation across individuals.

We run this regression on the subset of our database consisting of individuals who ran multiple marathons, which gave us {{ nobs }} observations. We estimate that wearing Vaporfly shoes causes a {{ regcoef|floatformat:3 }} percent decrease in marathon finish time. Again, this estimate is likely inaccurate due to selection bias in the subset of people who chose to purchase Vaporfly shoes, among other factors.
